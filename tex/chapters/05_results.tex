\section{Baseline: Financial Determinants of Volatility}
\label{sec:baseline}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Before introducing textual variables, we establish the predictive
power of standard financial determinants over future stock return
volatility.  Table~\ref{tab:baseline} reports OLS estimates of post-filing
annualised volatility on progressively richer sets of financial controls,
with two-digit SIC industry and fiscal-year fixed effects absorbed in all
specifications and standard errors clustered at the firm level.

\input{tables/baseline_regression}

\paragraph{Volatility persistence.}
Column~(1) includes only lagged volatility ($\sigma_{i,t}$) alongside the
fixed effects.  Its coefficient of $0.67$ ($t = 32.0$) confirms the
well-documented persistence of firm-level return volatility: a one
percentage-point increase in last year's annualised volatility is
associated with a two-thirds percentage-point increase in next year's
volatility.  Even this parsimonious specification, combined with industry
and year fixed effects, explains roughly 63\% of the variation in
post-filing volatility, indicating that the bulk of the predictable
component of volatility is mechanical.

\paragraph{Firm size and capital structure.}
Column~(2) adds firm size, measured as the natural logarithm of total
assets, and leverage, defined as total liabilities over total assets.
Size enters with a negative and highly significant coefficient
($-0.016$, $t = -7.3$): all else equal, larger firms exhibit lower
future volatility, consistent with their greater diversification, deeper
analyst coverage, and more liquid stock markets
\parencite{campbell2014}.  Leverage is economically negligible and
statistically insignificant ($t = 0.3$), likely because the
industry fixed effects already absorb much of the cross-sectional
variation in capital structure---leverage norms differ markedly by
industry---and because the leverage--volatility link operates partly
through the lagged-volatility channel (more leveraged firms had higher
past volatility).

\paragraph{Profitability and growth.}
The full specification in Column~(3) adds return on assets and asset
growth.  ROA carries a large negative coefficient ($-0.226$, $t = -8.0$):
more profitable firms face lower future volatility, reflecting both
lower fundamental uncertainty and a reduced probability of financial
distress.  Asset growth enters positively ($+0.027$, $t = 3.2$),
consistent with the intuition that rapid expansion---whether through
investment or acquisition---introduces additional uncertainty about
future cash flows.  The incremental adjusted~$R^2$ from these two
variables is modest (0.651 vs.\ 0.639), indicating that profitability
and growth refine the prediction beyond what size and past volatility
already capture, but do not dominate it.

\paragraph{Benchmark for textual analysis.}
The full baseline model explains 65.1\% of the variation in post-filing
volatility.  This adjusted~$R^2$ serves as the benchmark against which
subsequent models---augmented with textual features and the divergence
measure---will be evaluated.  Any incremental explanatory power from
narrative disclosures must exceed this already high bar, which is
consistent with the view that financial-statement variables and
volatility persistence jointly account for the majority of predictable
firm-level risk.
