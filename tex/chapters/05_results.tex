\section{Descriptive Statistics}
\label{sec:descriptive}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Table~\ref{tab:summary} reports summary statistics for the regression
sample.  The dependent variable, post-filing annualised volatility, has a
mean of about 0.33 and a standard deviation of 0.17, showing large
cross-sectional differences in firm-level risk.  The interquartile range
(0.22 to 0.39) points to a right-skewed distribution, consistent with
occasional spikes during market stress periods such as 2020.

Lagged volatility has a nearly identical distribution, as expected for a
persistent series.  Firm size, measured as $\ln(\text{Total Assets})$,
ranges from mid-sized (P25~$\approx$~22.1) to large companies
(P75~$\approx$~24.2).  Leverage centres around 0.61 with moderate spread.
ROA has a mean near 0.06, and asset growth averages about 0.12 with a
long right tail driven by acquisitive firms.

\input{tables/summary_statistics}

Table~\ref{tab:correlation} presents pairwise Pearson correlations.
Volatility and its lag are strongly positively correlated ($r = 0.61$),
confirming persistence.  Firm size correlates negatively with volatility
($r \approx -0.32$), in line with the well-known size--risk relationship.
ROA also correlates negatively with volatility ($r \approx -0.33$),
while leverage and asset growth show weaker univariate links.
None of the pairwise correlations among the explanatory variables
exceed 0.40 in absolute value, reducing multicollinearity concerns
(variance inflation factors are below 1.4 for all regressors).

\section{Baseline: Financial Determinants of Volatility}
\label{sec:baseline}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Before adding textual variables, we first measure the predictive power
of standard financial variables for future stock return volatility.
Table~\ref{tab:baseline} reports OLS estimates of post-filing annualised
volatility on progressively richer sets of financial controls, with
two-digit SIC industry and fiscal-year fixed effects absorbed in all
specifications and standard errors clustered by firm.

\input{tables/baseline_regression}

\paragraph{Volatility persistence.}
Column~(1) includes only lagged volatility ($\sigma_{i,t}$) alongside the
fixed effects.  Its coefficient of $0.67$ ($t = 33.5$) confirms the
well-known persistence of firm-level return volatility: a one
percentage-point rise in last year's annual volatility is associated with
about a two-thirds percentage-point rise in next year's volatility.  Even
this simple specification, combined with industry and year fixed effects,
explains about 63\% of the variation in post-filing volatility, showing
that most of the predictable part of volatility is mechanical.

\paragraph{Firm size and capital structure.}
Column~(2) adds firm size, measured as the natural logarithm of total
assets, and leverage (total liabilities over total assets).  Size has a
negative and highly significant coefficient ($-0.017$, $t = -7.4$):
larger firms have lower future volatility, consistent with the
well-known size--volatility relationship \parencite{chi2017}.  Leverage
is economically negligible and statistically insignificant ($t = 0.17$),
probably because the industry fixed effects already absorb much of the
cross-sectional variation in capital structure.

\paragraph{Profitability and growth.}
The full specification in Column~(3) adds return on assets and asset
growth.  ROA has a large negative coefficient ($-0.224$,
$t = -7.9$): more profitable firms face lower future volatility.  Asset
growth enters positively ($+0.026$, $t = 3.1$), suggesting that fast
expansion goes with higher subsequent uncertainty.

The gain in adjusted~$R^2$ from these two variables is modest
(0.654 vs.\ 0.643), meaning that profitability and growth refine the
prediction beyond what size and past volatility already capture, but do
not dominate it.

\paragraph{Benchmark for textual analysis.}
The full baseline model explains 65.4\% of the variation in post-filing
volatility.  This adjusted~$R^2$ serves as the benchmark against which
we will evaluate later models that add textual features and the
divergence measure.  Any incremental explanatory power from narrative
disclosures must beat this already high bar, which is consistent with
the idea that financial-statement variables and volatility persistence
together account for most of the predictable firm-level risk.
