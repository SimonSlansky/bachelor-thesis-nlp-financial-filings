\section{Sample Selection}
\label{sec:sample_selection}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

We start with the sample of 1{,}000 largest U.S.\ public companies by market
capitalisation, taken from the SEC EDGAR company-tickers file, and
apply three filters.  First, we keep only entities the SEC classifies
as ``operating,'' which drops exchange-traded funds, closed-end funds,
blank-cheque companies, and shell entities.  Second, we remove firms
in SIC codes~6000--6999 (finance, insurance, and real estate), whose
financial statements are structured differently from those of
industrial firms---a standard restriction in the accruals and
earnings-quality literature \parencite{sloan1996,francis2005}.  Third,
we require at least five years of machine-readable XBRL data from
10-K filings and 90\% valid stock-return coverage, so that there is
enough within-firm variation for fixed-effects estimation.  After
these filters, the sample contains roughly 540 non-financial firms
observed over fiscal years 2010--2024, giving about 6{,}400 firm-year
observations.

\paragraph{Sample size.}
We deliberately focus on large, liquid firms rather than the full
EDGAR universe for three reasons.  First, our regressions use firm
fixed effects with standard errors clustered by firm.  As
\textcite{petersen2009} shows, the reliability of cluster-robust
standard errors depends on the number of clusters---here about
540---not on total observations, and 540~clusters are well above the
threshold for reliable inference.  Second, the textual-analysis part
of the study requires downloading, parsing, and scoring every 10-K in
the sample.  Expanding to several thousand firms would greatly
increase computation time without a proportional gain in statistical
power.  Third, smaller firms tend to have shorter XBRL histories
(mandatory electronic filing was phased in by firm size), more tagging
errors, and thinner trading, all of which add noise to financial
ratios and return-based variables.

The sample size is in line with prior work combining textual and
financial analysis of annual filings.
\textcite{campbell2014} study about 7{,}000 firm-years of risk-factor
disclosures from large U.S.\ firms; \textcite{loughran2011} use a
broader set of roughly 50{,}000 firm-years, but their goal---validating
a sentiment dictionary---calls for maximal cross-sectional coverage,
whereas our divergence measure is estimated within firms over time.

\section{Filing Frequency}
\label{sec:annual_vs_quarterly}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Before moving to variable construction, it is worth explaining why this
study uses annual 10-K filings instead of quarterly 10-Q reports.
Quarterly data would roughly triple the sample, but three practical
problems---shown in Tables~\ref{tab:xbrl_coverage}--\ref{tab:text_availability}---ruled
out that approach.

\paragraph{Financial data gaps in Q4.}
10-Q filings cover only Q1 through Q3; the fourth quarter has to be
backed out as the difference between the annual figure and the sum of
the three preceding quarters. Table~\ref{tab:q4_reliability} shows
that complete Q1--Q3 XBRL data exist for about 80\% of firm-years in
net income and operating income, but for only 7\% in operating cash
flow---because most firms report cumulative year-to-date cash flows
rather than standalone quarterly amounts.  When both an explicit Q4
fact and the implied residual are available, about 11\% of observations
differ by more than 1\% of the annual total.  Since accruals depend
directly on operating cash flow, using quarterly data would have made
hypothesis~H3 untestable.

\input{tables/q4_reliability}

\paragraph{Missing risk-factor text in 10-Q.}
SEC Regulation S-K only requires firms to update risk factors in
quarterly filings when ``material changes'' have occurred since the
last 10-K.  In practice, just over half the 10-Q filings in our pilot
sample (15~firms, 90~filings) contained a real risk-factor section;
the rest had only a one-sentence reference back to the annual report
(Table~\ref{tab:text_availability}).  This held across sectors---technology,
healthcare, consumer goods, energy---with few exceptions.  Building
$\Delta$Risk, which enters both H1 and H2, is therefore not feasible
at a quarterly frequency.

\paragraph{Chosen frequency.}
For these reasons---and following prior textual-analysis work based on
10-K filings \parencite{loughran2011,li2008,blankespoor2020}---we use
an annual panel. The XBRL coverage comparison
(Table~\ref{tab:xbrl_coverage}) and a consolidated summary
(Table~\ref{tab:annual_vs_quarterly}) are in
Appendix~\ref{app:diagnostics}.

\section{XBRL Tag Consistency}
\label{sec:xbrl_tags}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

A practical problem with the XBRL Company Facts API is that a single
accounting concept can appear under different US-GAAP taxonomy tags.
For example, operating cash flow may be reported as either
\texttt{NetCashProvided\-ByUsed\-InOperating\-Activities} (total, including
discontinued operations) or
\texttt{...ContinuingOperations} (continuing operations only).
Net income may be tagged as \texttt{NetIncomeLoss}, \texttt{ProfitLoss},
or \texttt{IncomeLossFrom\-ContinuingOperations}.
Stockholders' equity can include or exclude the non-controlling
interest.  These tags overlap a lot but are not identical: mixing them
within a single firm's time series could create artificial breaks,
while picking one tag globally would lose firms that consistently
report under the other.

We handle this with a two-tier \emph{tag-harmonisation} procedure.

\paragraph{Equivalence groups.}
When two tags differ only in taxonomy version---not in economic
meaning---we let both coexist within a firm's time series.
Specifically, the two operating-cash-flow tags and the pair
\texttt{NetIncomeLoss}/\texttt{ProfitLoss} are treated as
interchangeable.  The third net-income variant,
\texttt{IncomeLossFromContinuingOperations}, is \emph{not} included
because it deliberately excludes discontinued operations and can
differ materially.

To check this classification empirically, we queried the SEC EDGAR
Company~Facts API for all 550 sample firms and found 6{,}113
firm-year periods where both tags of a pair were reported at the same
time.  For operating cash flow, 53.5\% of the 1{,}352 overlap
observations were numerically identical, and the median difference
scaled by total assets was 0.000~pp; even at the 90th~percentile the
bias was only 1.5~pp.  For net income, 25.5\% of the 4{,}761 overlaps
were exact matches; the median ROA bias was 0.033~pp, with 92.5\% of
observations below 1.0~pp.  The cases with meaningful differences
almost always involve firms with material non-controlling interests
(e.g.\ master limited partnerships), which our priority rule already
handles by preferring the parent-company tag when both are available.

\paragraph{Per-firm tag locking.}
For metrics where the alternative tags \emph{are} economically distinct
(stockholders' equity with vs.\ without NCI; \texttt{IncomeLoss\-From\-%
ContinuingOperations} vs.\ \texttt{NetIncomeLoss}), we apply strict
per-firm tag locking: we keep the tag each firm uses in the most fiscal
years and set values from other tags to missing.  This ensures
within-firm time-series consistency, which is the key requirement for
panel regressions that identify effects from within-firm variation.

Combining equivalence groups and per-firm locking raises the
complete-case rate for the six regression controls (\textit{roa},
\textit{accruals}, \textit{leverage}, \textit{asset\_growth},
\textit{current\_ratio}, \textit{log\_total\_assets}) from 78.9\% to
92.7\% of firm-year observations.  This recovers 988 accruals values,
828 operating-cash-flow values, and 292 net-income values that would
otherwise be lost.

\paragraph{Residual limitation.}
The accruals ratio $(\text{NI} - \text{OCF}) / \text{TA}$ needs NI
and OCF to measure income and cash flow at the \emph{same} scope.
Our procedure enforces consistency within each metric but not across
metrics: about 4\% of firms lock to the continuing-operations OCF tag
while using total net income.  We consider this acceptable because
the affected share is small, winsorisation at the 1st and 99th
percentiles clips extreme values, and the same kind of cross-tag
mismatch appears in comparable large-scale XBRL studies
\parencite{chen2019}.

Per-firm tag choices and the overlap diagnostic are reported in
Appendix~\ref{app:variables}
(Table~\ref{tab:tag_provenance}).

\section{Textual Data Extraction}
\label{sec:text_extraction}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Several hypotheses in this study depend on the narrative content of
10-K filings---specifically Item~1A \emph{Risk Factors} and Item~7
\emph{Management's Discussion and Analysis of Financial Condition and
Results of Operations} (MD\&A).  This section describes how we find
and extract those sections from the raw HTML filings on SEC EDGAR.

\subsection{Data Source and Filing Population}
\label{ssec:text_source}

All filings come directly from the SEC EDGAR Archives via the
Submissions~API, which links each company's Central Index Key (CIK)
to the primary documents of its 10-K filings.  From the panel of
about 6{,}700 firm-year observations, we drop 10-K/A amendments.
Amendments typically consist of a cover page and a single restated
exhibit and do not contain the full narrative sections of the
original 10-K.  Financial data for amended fiscal years are kept,
since the XBRL pipeline already picks up the amended figures when
available (Section~\ref{sec:xbrl_tags}).  Our extraction thus
targets only original 10-K filings.

\subsection{Section Identification}
\label{ssec:section_id}

Unlike structured XBRL data, the narrative parts of 10-K filings sit
inside HTML with no standardised tagging.  A raw filing can span tens
of thousands of lines, with section headers rendered in many different
ways---CSS bold, explicit \texttt{<font>} tags, inline uppercase text,
or table-based layouts---and mixed in with a table of contents (TOC),
running headers, and inline cross-references (e.g.\ ``see Item~1A'').
These features make simple keyword search on plain text unreliable,
because it confuses TOC entries, page headers, and inline mentions
with actual section boundaries \parencite{dyer2017}.

We use an HTML-aware extraction procedure with four stages:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Header detection.}  We walk through the HTML DOM at
    the block level (\texttt{<p>}, \texttt{<div>}, \texttt{<td>},
    \texttt{<h1>}--\texttt{<h6>}) and test each element against a
    set of regex patterns for the target sections (e.g.\
    \texttt{Item\textbackslash s+1[\textbackslash s.]*A} for
    Item~1A).  A candidate counts only if it meets at least one
    formatting rule: bold styling (CSS \texttt{font-weight:
    bold} or \texttt{700}), ALL-CAPS text, or large font size
    ($\geq$12\,pt via CSS or legacy \texttt{<font size$\geq$3>}).
    This filters out inline cross-references, which sit in normal
    body-text paragraphs with no special formatting.  Elements
    containing a visible hyperlink (an \texttt{<a>} tag with an
    \texttt{href}) are also rejected, as these are TOC entries
    pointing to the real section.

  \item \textbf{Position marking.}  Each detected header gets a
    unique marker character inserted into the DOM before the document
    is flattened to plain text.  The markers record the character
    offset of every confirmed header in the resulting text stream.

  \item \textbf{Candidate selection.}  For each target section, we
    filter all marker positions by a subtitle keyword check (e.g.\
    ``risk'' for Item~1A, ``management'' or ``discussion'' for
    Item~7) within 300~characters of the marker.  A TOC-cluster
    rule then drops any candidate whose position is within
    300~characters of three or more headers from different items---a
    pattern typical of tables of contents.  From the remaining
    candidates, we pick the one followed by the longest text span
    before the next section header.

  \item \textbf{Section boundary.}  The section text runs from the
    selected header to the first occurrence of the next expected Item
    header (Item~1B, 1C, or~2 for Item~1A; Item~7A or~8 for Item~7).
    Sections shorter than 200~words are treated as stubs and trigger
    a fallback procedure.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Exhibit Fallback}
\label{ssec:exhibit_fallback}

A common practice in 10-K filings---especially older ones and firms
that publish a standalone annual report---is to replace an Item with
a short stub such as ``This information is incorporated by reference
from Exhibit~13.''  When the extracted section has fewer than
200~words and the text matches an incorporation-by-reference pattern,
the parser finds the referenced exhibit number (defaulting to
Exhibit~13, the annual report to shareholders) and downloads the
matching HTML document from the filing index.  The section
identification procedure is then rerun on the exhibit.  Because
exhibits often skip the formal ``Item~X'' prefix, the parser also
matches title-only patterns (e.g.\ ``Risk Factors'', ``Management's
Discussion and Analysis'') as a backup detection layer for both the
main document and exhibits.

\subsection{Post-Processing}
\label{ssec:text_postprocessing}

The raw text from the HTML-to-plain-text conversion keeps several
layout artefacts from the original filing.  Before computing any
textual features, each extracted section goes through a cleaning step
that removes three types of noise:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Running page headers.}  Many filings embed
    ``\textit{$\langle$page number$\rangle$~Table of Contents}''
    strings at page boundaries.  A regular expression strips any
    line matching a one- to three-digit number followed by
    ``Table of Contents.''
  \item \textbf{Standalone page numbers.}  After removing the full
    header pattern, some filings still contain bare page numbers on
    otherwise empty lines.  These are removed by a second pass that
    deletes any line consisting solely of a one- to three-digit
    integer.
  \item \textbf{Non-breaking spaces.}  HTML \texttt{\&nbsp;} entities
    (Unicode~\texttt{U+00A0}) are replaced with standard spaces to
    ensure consistent tokenisation in downstream word-count and
    dictionary-based measures.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent
The cleaning rules are intentionally conservative: they target only
patterns that cannot realistically be part of meaningful text and are
applied after section boundaries have already been set, so they do
not interfere with header detection.

\subsection{Coverage and Limitations}
\label{ssec:text_coverage}

We validated the extraction pipeline on two samples: the 50~most
recent filings in the panel (fiscal year 2024) and a stratified
random sample of 100~filings covering all fiscal years 2010--2024
(20~per era).  On the recent sample, the parser successfully
extracted both sections for all 50~filings (100\%).  On the
cross-era sample, 94~of~100 filings yielded both sections.  The
six failures break down as follows:

\begin{itemize}
  \item Three 10-K/A amendments that passed the initial filter because
    the panel did not yet carry a form-type indicator at the time of
    testing (since corrected);
  \item Two filings whose annual report exhibit was filed as a PDF
    rather than HTML, making it inaccessible to an HTML parser;
  \item One filing (9.3\,MB of HTML) that contained no bold, large-font,
    or uppercase cues whatsoever, rendering header detection infeasible.
\end{itemize}

\noindent
None of these failures is related to firm characteristics in any
systematic way.  On the full panel---after dropping amendments---we
expect a coverage rate of roughly 96--97\%, with missing values
treated as NaN in the textual variable construction.

\paragraph{AI-assisted quality audit.}

To check not just coverage but also content quality, we ran an
independent audit using a large language model (Gemini~2.5 Flash,
temperature~$= 0$).  Recent work by \textcite{gilardi2023} shows
that LLM-based annotation matches or beats the accuracy of trained
human crowd-workers on text-classification tasks, which supports
using a language model as an independent extraction judge.
A~stratified random sample of 100~filings was drawn---40~sections
per era across five strata (2010--2012, 2013--2015, 2016--2018,
2019--2021, 2022--2024)---giving 200~section-level evaluations
(Item~1A and Item~7 for each filing).
The sample size is above the minimum of~80~coding units recommended
by \textcite{krippendorff2019} for content-analysis reliability
testing.  At the observed pass rates, the Wilson~95\,\%
confidence interval half-width stays below about 3~percentage points
\parencite{agresti1998}, which is precise enough for a clear quality
claim.  The 100~filings cover roughly~18\,\% of the 540-firm panel
and span the full history of SEC filing formats.

For each filing, both extracted sections were sent to the model in a
single prompt along with a structured evaluation rubric.  The model
judged each section on five criteria---correct section identity,
start-boundary accuracy, end-boundary accuracy, content quality
(no residual HTML tags, page headers, or garbled data), and
completeness (full narrative, not a stub or truncated fragment)---using
a three-level scale: \emph{Pass} (criterion clearly met),
\emph{Minor} (small issue that would not meaningfully affect NLP
analysis), and \emph{Fail} (issue that would distort results).  We
report two aggregate metrics: a strict rate (only Pass) and a lenient
rate (Pass~+~Minor), each with Wilson~95\,\% confidence intervals
\parencite{agresti1998}.

Table~\ref{tab:ai_validation} shows the results.  On every individual
criterion, the lenient pass rate (Pass~+~Minor) is above 95\%,
confirming that the parser finds the right section with accurate
boundaries and produces clean, complete text.  Even under the strict
metric---counting only an unqualified Pass---rates stay above 94\% on
each criterion.  The overall lenient rate across all five criteria at
once is 93.5\% (187/200; 95\,\%~CI: 89.2--96.2\,\%).

Of the 13~sections with at least one Fail, five were complete
extraction failures (empty output) caused by non-standard HTML layouts
or parser recursion limits; four had wrong section boundaries (content
from an adjacent Item was included); and the remaining four were
flagged only for low word counts suggesting possible truncation.
Item~7 extractions do slightly better than Item~1A (lenient pass rates
of 96\% vs.\ 91\%), most likely because Item~7 has a cleaner stop
boundary (Item~7A or Item~8), while Item~1A sometimes picks up text
from the preceding Item~1.

Failures also cluster in older filings: 5~of the 40~sections from
2010--2012 were flagged versus only 1~of~40 from 2022--2024.  This
reflects the gradual standardisation of SEC filing HTML over the
sample period and confirms that the remaining failures are not tied
to firm characteristics but are instead an artefact of filing-format
variation in the early XBRL era.

\paragraph{Robustness of the LLM-based audit.}
We use Google Gemini~2.5~Flash (accessed through its
OpenAI-compatible API) with temperature set to~0 for deterministic
results.  Several design choices support the reliability of this
approach.  First, \textcite{gilardi2023} show that LLM-based
annotation matches or beats trained crowd-workers on
text-classification tasks, giving methodological backing for using a
language model as an independent judge.  Second, the rubric is
built around concrete, verifiable criteria---correct section identity,
boundary accuracy, absence of HTML artefacts, word-count
thresholds---that leave little room for subjective interpretation.
Third, each judgment covers a single document with a deterministic
prompt; the model does not need to compare across filings or
aggregate statistics.  Fourth, spot-checking the model's Fail
verdicts against manual inspection confirmed agreement in every case
examined (e.g.\ the PH~2015 and FE~2010 empty extractions, the
ROP~2019 and PEP~2017 boundary errors).  Together, a well-defined
rubric, zero temperature, and verifiable failure modes make the
audit reproducible: a different evaluator---human or model---applying
the same criteria should reach the same conclusions.

\paragraph{Relation to prior approaches.}
Our extraction strategy follows the general approach of
\textcite{dyer2017}, who document large variation in the HTML
structure of SEC filings and recommend DOM-based parsing instead of
regular expressions on raw text.  Compared with keyword-on-plain-text
methods used in earlier work
\parencite{li2008,loughran2011,campbell2014}, our procedure avoids
false positives from TOC entries and inline mentions, which
\textcite{dyer2017} flag as a major source of measurement error in
automated section extraction.  Compared with XBRL-tagged textual
blocks (e.g.\ \texttt{us-gaap:RiskFactorsTextBlock}), which became
available with Inline XBRL adoption, our method covers a wider time
span: structured textual tags are inconsistently filled before 2018,
while the HTML-based approach works across the full 2010--2024 window.
